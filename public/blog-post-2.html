Journal/Playbook

Running a values refresh without making everyone hate values.

The six-week playbook we've seen work at Figment, Dialogue, and fourteen other Canadian teams — including the exact Monday-morning agenda we recommend for the kickoff session.

J
Jordan Tremblay CTO & co-founder
Jan 28, 2025 8 min read

Every values refresh I have ever been near has followed the same terrible arc. A leadership offsite produces a list of words. The words arrive at the company in a deck. There is an all-hands where they are explained. Somebody makes wallpaper. For two weeks the words are in every meeting. After a month, nobody remembers them. The org quietly returns to running on vibes and whatever the founders actually say in 1:1s. Eighteen months later, a new CHRO joins and announces another values refresh.

This is not a People problem. It's a process problem. The process almost every company uses is optimized for producing a clean deliverable, when what it needs to produce is a shared vocabulary. Those are different things, and conflating them is why nobody remembers the last five posters in your kitchen.

We've now watched our customers run values refreshes a lot. Some of them go great. Some of them are catastrophic. The pattern that separates them is legible and, I think, teachable. Here's the playbook.

The shape: six weeks, three phases.

Most values refreshes are run as a single two-week sprint. This is too fast. The process has to let ideas breathe, and two weeks means you're using the first draft of everyone's first thought. Six weeks is long enough to let the writing get sharp and short enough that the company doesn't lose the plot.

1Listen

Gather the raw material.

Before anyone picks words, get real. 1:1s with 20–30 people across levels and functions. Ask what they think the company actually values — not aspirationally, but based on what gets someone promoted and what gets someone fired. You'll hear truths.

2Listen

Read the artifacts.

All-hands transcripts, founder letters, the company chat #general. Whatever the CEO actually rewards in public is a value. What language recurs when people praise someone? That's your raw corpus.

3Draft

First pass — small group.

4–6 people, offsite or at least out of the office, one full day. Draft 8–12 candidate values in plain English. No corporate bingo. No "drive world-class excellence." If you wouldn't say it at the dinner table, cross it out.

4Draft

Pressure-test with skeptics.

Show the draft to the three most cynical people in your company. Actual cynics. Ask them to argue each word. What survives their red pen is worth keeping; what doesn't wasn't going to survive anyway.

5Share

Workshop with managers.

Not a reveal — a collaborative session. Your managers are the ones who will actually operationalize these values. Their fingerprints need to be on it. Expect 3–5 of your words to change or die here.

6Share

All-hands, then into the tool.

Announce at a company meeting with examples — real stories of the values showing up. Then, and this is the part everyone skips, load them into whatever recognition, review, and onboarding systems you use the same week. If the values aren't operationalized immediately, they stop existing.

Values that don't touch a system within two weeks of launch will revert. I've never seen an exception.

The part where everyone screws up: the examples.

The single biggest difference between the values refreshes that stick and the ones that don't is whether you've paired every value with a concrete, specific, embarrassing-in-a-good-way story. "Creative courage" means nothing. "Creative courage is what Leila did when she killed the Q3 launch two weeks before it shipped because the positioning wasn't right, and the CEO backed her, and we relaunched it in November and it doubled the forecast" — that lands.

For every value, you need at least three of those. Your job in weeks 1 and 2 is to find them. They should be recent. They should feature at least one person who is not an executive. And — this is non-negotiable — they should include stories where the value cost somebody something. A value that doesn't have a cost isn't a value; it's a preference.

The Monday-morning kickoff agenda.

The single most important meeting in this whole process is the day-one offsite in week 3. Here is the exact agenda we recommend. It's 4 hours with a proper lunch. Do not compress this.

Values draft — day 1

4h offsite · 4–6 people · paper & sharpies only · phones in a box
09:00
What the company is not.15 min, silent writing. Each person lists five things we are emphatically not. Read aloud. No discussion. Just inventory.
09:15
Stories round.45 min. Each person brings 2 recent moments where someone did something that made you proud of the company. What exactly happened? What did they choose? Take notes on the verbs.
10:00
Verb inventory.20 min. Write every verb from the stories on the wall. Cluster. Don't name anything yet. Just look at the clusters.
10:20
Break.15 min. Walk outside. Don't talk about work.
10:35
First names.60 min. Working from the verb clusters, name 8–12 candidate values. Each name must be something you'd say to a friend. Kill anything that sounds like a consulting deck.
11:35
The "cost" test.30 min. For each value: what does it cost the company to live by it? If the answer is "nothing," kill it. Values that are free are just aesthetics.
12:05
Lunch.Real one. 60 min minimum. Do not work-lunch.
13:05
The one-line test.45 min. Each candidate value gets a one-line paraphrase in plain speech. "Creative courage" → "We'd rather be wrong in a new way than right in the old way." The one-liner is what will actually travel.
13:50
Trim.30 min. You will have 10–12 candidates. Get to 5–7. You will fight about this. Good.
14:20
Exit ticket.10 min. Each person privately writes: which of these is still bullshit? Read aloud. That's your week-4 homework.

The most important thing is that you stop picking seven.

Everybody picks seven. The magic number is not seven. The magic number is probably four or five. Seven values means nobody can list them from memory, and if nobody can list them from memory they're not going to show up in anybody's performance review or 1:1 or recognition. They're going to live on a wall.

The customers of ours that have four or five values can, universally, recite them without thinking. The ones with seven or eight cannot. This isn't complicated.

Rule of thumb

If your CEO can't list all of the values, unprompted, in the right order, while on a walk — you have too many. Cut until they can.

The operational half that nobody does.

Okay. Your values are drafted. Pressure-tested. Workshopped. Announced. Now — and this is the part that matters as much as all the other weeks combined — you need to get them into the three systems where they will live or die:

  1. Recognition. Every kudos in the company should be taggable to a value. This is a ~2 hour change in most tools (we took 20 minutes in Bryte Rewards, because we would say that). The value tag on the card is the single most effective way to keep them in peoples' mouths.
  2. Performance reviews. Each value gets a question. "Give me an example of where this person demonstrated Creative Courage this half." If your review template doesn't include the values, the values are not operational.
  3. Onboarding. Day one, week one. Not as a deck slide — as a conversation. A manager tells a new hire the story behind each value. They write down which one they think they'll lean on most. That note lives in their file.

Do those three things within two weeks of launch and your values will stick. Skip any of them and by quarter three they will have returned to being wallpaper.

The honest bit.

Here's the part I always tell customers at the end of this playbook: the single best predictor of whether a values refresh will stick is not the quality of the words, or the rigour of the process, or the number of stories you collected. It's whether the CEO can be trusted to actually hold the line when the values are inconvenient.

If your CEO has written on the wall "We will not ship what we wouldn't be proud of" and then six months later announces a Q4 push where you ship something you're not proud of — you don't have a values problem. You have a leadership problem, and no amount of recognition software, mine included, is going to fix that.

But if you've got a CEO who'll take the hit when a value costs them something — you've got the most important ingredient. The rest of this playbook is just the packaging.
